Extranet

A controlled private network that extends intranet access to authorised external parties — suppliers, partners, or customers.

Definition

An extranet is a controlled private network that allows access to an organisation's internal intranet by authorised external users, such as business partners, suppliers, vendors, contractors, or customers. It serves as a bridge between a private intranet and the public Internet, providing selective external access to specific internal resources.

An extranet uses the same Internet protocols (TCP/IP, HTTP) as the Internet and intranet, but employs authentication, authorisation, and encryption to control access and secure data transmission — typically through VPNs (Virtual Private Networks) or secure login portals.

How an Extranet Works

An extranet typically involves three components:

  1. The Host Intranet — The organisation's internal private network containing resources to be shared.
  2. The Secure Gateway — A firewall, VPN, or secure web portal that controls and authenticates external access.
  3. Authorised External Users — Partners, suppliers, or customers granted specific, limited access credentials.

Selected external parties
Security

B2B portals, supply chain

Common Uses of Extranets

  • Supply Chain Management — Allows suppliers to check inventory levels, submit invoices, and track orders.
  • Customer Portals — Gives customers access to their account information, order history, and support.
  • Partner Collaboration — Enables joint project teams across organisations to share documents and progress.
  • Healthcare Networks — Connects hospitals, clinics, and insurance providers for secure patient data exchange.
  • Financial Services — Banks share account information with third-party financial applications.
Extranets became prominent in the late 1990s as businesses recognised the value of extending their internal systems to partners without exposing the entire intranet to the public Internet.

Advantages & Disadvantages

Advantages: Streamlines B2B communication, reduces operational costs, improves collaboration, enables real-time data sharing with partners, and enhances customer service through self-service portals.

Disadvantages: Higher setup and maintenance costs than an intranet alone, increased security attack surface, requires careful access management to prevent over-sharing of sensitive data, and dependence on Internet connectivity for external access.
